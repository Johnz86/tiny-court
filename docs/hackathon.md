# Document 1: Build Small Hackathon Brief

## Event Overview

The Build Small Hackathon is a Hugging Face and Gradio hackathon about building useful, delightful AI applications with small models.

The central idea is simple:

Build a Hugging Face Space powered by models under 32B parameters.

The hackathon intentionally pushes builders away from giant-model thinking. The goal is not to build another generic AI wrapper or B2B SaaS demo. The goal is to build something small, focused, polished, useful, strange, joyful, or personally meaningful.

The hackathon asks participants to return to the feeling of early AI experimentation: playful, practical, accessible, and fun to tinker with.

## Core Challenge

The challenge is to build a Gradio application hosted as a Hugging Face Space.

The application must respect three main constraints:

1. Small models only
   The total model size must be at or below 32 billion parameters.

2. Built on Gradio
   The submitted application must be a Gradio app hosted as a Hugging Face Space.

3. Show, don’t tell
   The submission must include a short demo video and a social-media post, not just code.

The hackathon rewards applications that can be understood quickly, demonstrated clearly, and experienced directly by users.

## Hackathon Timeline

The required final submission package includes:

# Track 1: Backyard AI

## Definition

Backyard AI is about solving a real problem for someone you actually know.

This could be:

* a neighbor,
* a parent,
* a friend,
* a local shop owner,
* a teacher,
* a small-business owner,
* a community organizer,
* a family member,
* someone in your daily life.

The app should make that person’s day measurably better.

This track is not about hypothetical users. It is about a specific person with a specific problem.

## What Backyard AI Rewards

Backyard AI is judged on:

* whether the problem is specific and real,
* whether the person actually used the app,
* whether the small-model constraint honestly fits the problem,
* whether the Gradio app is polished.

## What a Strong Backyard AI Submission Should Show

A strong Backyard AI project should show:

1. Who the user is
   For example: “A local café owner who manages shifts through messy WhatsApp messages.”

2. What the real problem is
   For example: “She spends 45 minutes every Sunday turning staff messages into a schedule.”

3. Why AI helps
   For example: “The app extracts availability, detects conflicts, and drafts a weekly roster.”

4. Why a small model is enough
   For example: “The task is structured extraction, summarization, and simple reasoning, not open-ended expert intelligence.”

5. Evidence that the person used it
   For example: screenshots, feedback, before/after time comparison, or a quote.

6. A polished Gradio experience
   The app should feel complete, guided, and easy to use.

## Best Mindset for Backyard AI

Think small, local, and real.

Do not ask:

“What impressive AI system can I build?”

Ask:

“Who near me has an annoying repeated problem that a small AI app could reduce?”

# Track 2: An Adventure in Thousand Token Wood

## Definition

An Adventure in Thousand Token Wood is about building something delightful that would not exist without AI.

This track is for:

* tiny games,
* strange toys,
* interactive stories,
* art experiments,
* playful agents,
* weird creative tools,
* surprising AI experiences.

The app should be joyful, original, and worth showing to a friend.

## What Thousand Token Wood Rewards

Thousand Token Wood is judged on:

* whether the app is genuinely delightful,
* whether the AI is load-bearing for the experience,
* whether the concept is original,
* whether the Gradio app is polished.

## What “AI Is Load-Bearing” Means

The AI should not be decorative.

A weak version is:

“The app has a normal game, and AI writes a generic description.”

A strong version is:

“The app only works because AI generates the witness testimony, surreal case files, strange world logic, personalized characters, or adaptive story events.”

The AI should be responsible for the fun part.

## Best Mindset for Thousand Token Wood

Think weird, memorable, and interactive.

Do not ask:

“What useful productivity app can I make?”

Ask:

“What is a tiny AI experience that would make someone laugh, smile, replay it, and send it to a friend?”

# Bonus Quests

The hackathon includes six optional bonus quests. They are not required, but they can add extra points to a submission.

## Off the Grid

The app uses no cloud APIs. The whole experience runs locally or directly through the model available to the app.

Focus: independence, local-first design, and self-contained execution.

## Well-Tuned

The app uses a fine-tuned model that the builder has published on Hugging Face.

Focus: task-specific quality and evidence that the model was adapted for the app.

## Off-Brand

The app has a custom frontend that goes beyond the default Gradio look.

Focus: visual identity, custom UI, and a memorable interface.

## Llama Champion

The model runs through the llama.cpp runtime.

Focus: efficient small-model execution.

## Sharing is Caring

The builder shares the agent trace on the Hugging Face Hub so others can learn from the process.

Focus: transparency and reproducibility.

## Field Notes

The builder writes a blog post or report about what they built and what they learned.

Focus: reflection, documentation, and learning in public.

# Strategic Takeaway

This hackathon is not only about model performance.

The winning entries will likely combine:

* a clear concept,
* a strong user experience,
* a focused small-model use case,
* a polished Gradio interface,
* a short and memorable demo,
* a submission story that judges can understand quickly.

For Backyard AI, the strongest proof is real-world usefulness.

For Thousand Token Wood, the strongest proof is delight.

For both tracks, the app must feel finished.
